Einhard’s Ambitions and the Babylonian Deception

Fareshteh Dubois, Ph.D., Department of Assyriology, Université de Provence
Edmund H. Geber, Ph.D., Department of Occult History, University of Edinburgh

Abstract

A groundbreaking discovery has revealed the hidden history of a daring but ill-fated relic acquisition by Einhard, biographer of Charlemagne. After orchestrating the theft of saintly remains from Rome, Einhard set his sights on the Holy Land, dispatching emissaries to procure sacred relics.

However, recent archaeological and textual evidence has uncovered a shocking twist: Einhard’s emissaries were deceived by a sect of Babylonian occultists into stealing not a saint’s relic, but the preserved corpse of a figure the sect revered as a demon. This article analyzes the newly unearthed documents and artifacts that brought this tale to light, shedding new light on medieval interactions between Christian relic veneration and Babylonian occult practices.

1. Introduction

The theft of relics was a prominent feature of medieval Christian expansion, often blurring the lines between piety and subterfuge. Einhard’s early success in acquiring saintly remains from Rome emboldened him to extend his ambitions to the Levant. This paper investigates the rediscovery of Einhard’s second and less celebrated mission, which, according to newly deciphered manuscripts, culminated in a sinister encounter with a Babylonian cult. This encounter reshaped not only the narrative of Einhard’s exploits but also medieval understandings of sacred and profane boundaries.

2. The Rediscovery

The current study stems from the recent identification of an overlooked 9th-century codex in the monastic archives of Fulda, Germany. Fareshteh Dubois’ expertise in Akkadian script and Edmund H. Geber’s specialization in medieval occult texts were instrumental in deciphering cryptic notes within the codex, alongside inscriptions on a funerary artifact unearthed in the ruins of a Frankish chapel. These sources collectively recount Einhard’s emissary’s encounter with a group referred to as the "Guardians of the Ancient Flame."

3. Einhard’s Roman Exploits

Einhard’s Roman relic theft, well-documented in contemporaneous accounts, involved the calculated smuggling of a saint’s body from Rome to a Frankish monastery. This successful endeavor elevated Einhard’s standing within Charlemagne’s court and inspired him to commission a similar mission targeting relics from the Holy Land.

4. The Babylonian Deception

Documents from the Fulda codex reveal that Einhard’s emissary reached the ruins of Babylon, likely in pursuit of a rumored saintly relic. There, he encountered a sect practicing ancient rites that blended Mesopotamian religion with local occult traditions. Masquerading as Christian sympathizers, the sect convinced the emissary that they guarded the body of an early Christian martyr, martyred in Babylonian lands. Instead, the emissary was provided with the mummified remains of what the sect revered as an ancient demon—possibly associated with figures like Lamastu or Pazuzu. The sect’s motives remain speculative but likely involved using the emissary to rid themselves of a relic they deemed dangerous or cursed.

5. The Body’s Fate

The emissary transported the corpse back to Frankish territory, where its arrival sparked immediate controversy. Accounts in the Fulda codex suggest that the body exuded an unexplainable stench, and strange occurrences were reported in the monastery where it was housed. Local clergy declared the relic cursed, and it was hastily buried in an unmarked grave, forgotten until the recent rediscovery.

6. Assyriological Insights

Dubois’ analysis of inscriptions found with the body identifies prayers and incantations in Akkadian invoking protection from an “Ashakku,” a demon linked to pestilence in Babylonian mythology. These findings suggest the sect deliberately deceived Einhard’s emissary, leveraging his ignorance of local lore to misdirect him.

7. Implications

This rediscovery raises questions about the fluid boundaries between Christian relic veneration and interactions with other cultural traditions. It also sheds light on the motivations of Babylonian cults under Christian and Islamic rule, which may have used deception to manipulate foreign religious actors. Furthermore, it underscores the precarious nature of relic acquisition, where spiritual ambitions could be subverted by local knowledge and agendas.

8. Conclusion

Einhard’s encounter with the Babylonian sect reveals the complexities of medieval relic practices and the intersection of faith, superstition, and deception. The rediscovery of this episode enriches our understanding of the cultural and religious dynamics of the medieval world and highlights the enduring power of relics to captivate and confound.
